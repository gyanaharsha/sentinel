Abstract Stolen credentials are the leading initial-access vector in confirmed breaches1, largely because 94% of leaked passwords are reused2. Checking a password against breach corpora is the standard defense — but naively, the check itself leaks the password. This page implements the k-anonymity range protocol: the password is hashed locally and only a 5-character prefix is ever transmitted. The claim is verifiable in your browser's network tab. Fig. 2 visualizes the live breach data each query returns.

§ 3

Method: how the check stays private

Hash locally. The browser computes the SHA-1 fingerprint of the password on your machine — 40 hexadecimal characters. The password itself never exists anywhere else. (Yes, SHA-1; see note 4 before objecting4.)

Ask by prefix. Only the first 5 characters go to the API. Roughly 800 leaked hashes share any given prefix, so the server sees a crowd, not a person — that crowd size is the k in k-anonymity. Responses are padded to a uniform size so that even traffic-length analysis learns nothing.

Compare locally. The full range returns and your browser checks, privately, whether your remaining 35 characters appear in it.

Instead of asking the librarian "do you have my diary?" — you ask for everything on shelf 5B and search the shelf yourself.
§ 4

Standards agree: NIST SP 800-63B, Rev. 4

The current US federal guidance (finalized 2025) rewrote the password rulebook5. This page implements its two central ideas — blocklist screening and length-first strength.

RuleWhat Rev. 4 says
15+ charsMinimum length when a password is the only authentication factor.
No composition rules"Shall not" require symbols/digits/case mixes — such rules push people into predictable patterns.
Blocklists mandatoryNew passwords must be screened against breached and common-password corpora. That screening is what Fig. 1 does.
No forced expiryRotate only on evidence of compromise — e.g., appearing in Fig. 1's verdict.
